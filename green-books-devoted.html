The Search for Perfect Awareness

On Devoted (2008)

We are neighbours of the uncanny. The names, cosmologies, and ritual worlds gathered in Devoted are not always mine, yet the gestures belong to a neighbourhood I recognise: the appointed act repeated until it begins to shape the person, the offering whose meaning lies partly in being offered, the sense that something has addressed us before we have chosen the terms of our answer. The book is concerned with lives ordered around such encounters, and its contributors write from within their devotions rather than observing them from a safe distance.

This is the book's chief strength. Its writers rarely stop to prove that they are reasonable, and they decline to write for the reader whom this essay, in the end, must answer: the one for whom a god must become a projection before it can be discussed, ritual a technique, and offering a means of regulating the self. They tell us what they do, whom they address, what their practice asks of them, and why they return. What emerges is not a shared doctrine but a series of lives formed around the possibility that the act is received, that repetition enters into relation, and that the world contains more agencies than that reader is prepared to admit.

The thought that followed me through the book concerned awareness. Roberto Calasso distinguishes ritual, which aims towards perfect awareness, from procedure, which tends towards automatism. It is an exact distinction for a time in which procedures govern so much of life, asking us to perform an ever-growing number of actions while attending less and less to what the actions mean. Yet the phrase perfect awareness also opens a difficulty. Awareness may become more concentrated without becoming more truthful. A person may perceive with extraordinary vividness and still misunderstand what has been perceived. The ordinary surface of things may give way without revealing what lies beneath it, and whatever appears may not bear the name first given to it. This became the question I carried through every contribution: when a practice opens a person, what receives the opening, and by what light is the address to be discerned? Everything that follows here, from the mirror-barrier to the Little Office and at last to Mary, answers to it.

The question became clearest for me in Alkistis Dimech's account of the mirror-barrier. Through disciplined repetition, she suggests, the body can cease to inspect itself from outside. The socially conditioned voice that ordinarily intervenes between impulse and movement begins to fall silent, and the body acquires another freedom.

I recognised the barrier immediately. In a connected life, the possible audience has entered the private room. Before an act is completed, one imagines how it might appear, how it might be explained, diagnosed, or mocked. One hears oneself praying as another person might hear it and sees oneself kneeling as another might see it. The act becomes divided between what it is and its anticipated reception.

Beneath the fear of appearing insincere lies another fear, perhaps the more powerful one: that the need for devotion will itself be taken as evidence that one is intellectually, professionally, or psychologically diminished. The problem is not confined to the internet, although the internet has made its workings difficult to ignore. Society has been carried inward until its imagined gaze begins to resemble an invisible witness. One no longer kneels before God while enduring the judgement of others; one kneels before their anticipated judgement while attempting to address God.

Dimech helped me understand that devotion may require a passage through this mirror. Yet the thought does not end with its breaking. Once the body is released from watching itself, what receives it? The removal of the social spectator creates an opening, but an opening is not yet freedom. The suspension of self-observation may release attention, though it may also make the person more available to whatever enters the cleared space. What matters is not that self-consciousness has been overcome, but the kind of relation that becomes possible when it is.

Jake Stratton-Kent's contribution led me towards the same question from another direction. He observes that remarkable spiritual work rests upon an ordinary and usually hidden cycle of devotional labour. Beneath the striking act lies the repeated act; beneath revelation lies return. Whatever else devotion may contain, it must survive the days on which nothing remarkable happens.

My own hidden cycle is the Little Office of the Blessed Virgin Mary. It receives me after my intentions have weakened and asks for no novelty, intensity, or convincing spiritual mood. It returns me to words that were not written for the present weather of my emotions, and in doing so releases me from the need to originate my prayer.

The canticles remain. The Magnificat does not wait until I am worthy to speak it. The Benedictus and the Nunc dimittis are already there, carrying their own time and asking me to enter it. Through repetition, a private life is gradually placed within scriptural time, while distraction, shame, enthusiasm, and fatigue move around the appointed words like weather over stone.

There is freedom in this, though it is not the freedom of having no form. It comes from no longer having to be the author of every spiritual movement. Simone Weil's language of decreation is helpful here, provided it is not taken to mean that the self must be made vacant, ready for whatever power might wish to enter. The surrender involved is more exact. It is the relinquishing of the claim to be one's own source, so that the person may be received into a mystery already under way.

The stability of the Office also guards against treating each movement of the psyche as revelation. Its words interpret me before I begin to interpret them. They neither deny experience nor permit experience to become its own final authority.

Cantillation, and the dulcimer on which I have begun to adapt the psalm tones, deepen this bodily form of repetition. Breath, pitch, tempo, and touch are given a path. There is a note to sound, a phrase to carry, and an ending towards which the voice may move. Technique does not decorate the prayer from outside. It helps the body remain within it.

Here the practices described in Devoted came closest to my own life. Its contributors know that repetition changes perception, that prescribed gestures may teach the body, and that sound can carry the person beyond the limits of passing feeling. This proximity did not make the different devotions seem equivalent. It made their differences more precise. Not every overcoming of the ego is decreation, and not every release from self-consciousness leads towards freedom. The question is what relation the practice establishes, whether it enlarges attention or narrows it, whether it deepens contact with reality or offers an escape from it, and whether the person becomes more capable of freedom and love or more dependent upon intensity.

My Christian difference does not begin by denying the seriousness of ritual or the reality of the invisible. It begins with the conviction that the circle of ritual, sacrifice, and return is open. It has been entered by an event it could neither summon nor contain. In the coming of the Son, the divine does not remain at the circumference as the power towards which offerings rise. It enters the circle, undergoes its violence, and opens recurrence towards fulfilment. I offer this as confession rather than conclusion. It is a wager the book cannot be asked to verify and I cannot demonstrate, and it is held in the same reserve I will shortly praise in Mary.

This does not make every ritual a concealed form of Christianity, nor every devotional presence another name for Christ. It means that no ritual world can be entirely self-enclosed, just as Christian practice itself cannot claim immunity from error, fantasy, or possession. Something may have exerted a real force upon the body and imagination while still being misunderstood. An encounter may be genuine as encounter without its first interpretation being complete. The invisible may be real without being innocent.

The book returned me, in this way, to the thought that devotion begins in address. Before it becomes a discipline selected by the self, something has already approached, whether as name, image, word, obligation, face, or presence. Practice is the continuing form of the answer. Devoted seemed strongest to me where it showed spiritual life not as self-expression or private symbolism, but as response.

Yet not every address deserves consent. A summons may be vocation or seduction; a presence may invite relation or capture. Sincerity cannot settle the matter, nor can intensity. This is where the question I have been carrying finds its Marian form. Mary is not an abstraction in my devotional life but someone addressed by name, borne in images, and returned to through feasts and hours. Yet her presence does not close upon itself. She receives and bears the Son. Her intimacy does not consume the person who approaches her, and her power does not depend upon possession. She is wholly present while remaining wholly referential.

Mary also helps me distinguish reserve from shame. Shame allows an imagined audience to determine what may be loved. Reserve protects a relation from compulsory exposure. She keeps and ponders, but she also sings aloud. The question is not whether devotion must be private or public, but how it may be spoken without becoming a performance of the self.

Several contributors to Devoted write about the difficulty of speaking at all. Richard Ward doubts not the reality of his devotion but his ability to communicate it without falsifying it. Ruby Sara finds a partial answer by allowing language itself to become part of the offering. Their contributions made me wonder what a faithful account of devotion might sound like. It would need to avoid romantic enlargement and embarrassed erasure alike. It would have to say: this is what I encountered; this is what I thought it meant; this is what remains; and this is how I understand it now.

That is also the purpose I imagine for these Green Books. They will not be reviews in the strict sense, nor attempts to provide a complete account of each book. They are records of thoughts opened by particular works, especially books concerned with ritual, secrecy, sacrifice, altered perception, and the human wish to approach what exceeds us. Such books need not be accepted whole in order to be read seriously, nor dismissed because their account of the invisible differs from mine. A record can be preserved without being left to interpret itself.

The world inhabited by the reader for whom gods are projections has dispersed devotion rather than abolished it. Its energies now flow across visibility, identity, politics, consumption, and the ceaseless production of content. Ritual, too, persists everywhere; repeated and formalised acts remain, though they now often reaffirm society itself rather than address anything beyond it. The question is not whether human beings repeat, but what their repetitions make present.

For me, the Little Office is no antiquarian refuge from that question. It is where the question becomes concrete. It offers another understanding of repetition and awareness, not as the conquest of an altered state but as participation in a form one does not possess.

The search for perfect awareness must therefore remain a search. Christian perfection is not the completion of perception as private property, nor the ability to see everything without remainder. It is participation in a light received rather than mastered. The aim is not to become invulnerably lucid, but to see more truthfully; to become open without becoming formless; to consent without being captured.

When Devoted is closed, its voices remain, with the practices, names, and encounters they describe, and with the questions they have opened. The record is preserved with those questions still standing, and the appointed canticle receives it without adjudicating it. Breath is gathered, the string is sounded, and the work of attention begins again.
